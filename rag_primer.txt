Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): A Primer

Large Language Models (LLMs) are trained on a fixed snapshot of data and cannot
know about information created after their training cutoff, nor can they see
private or domain-specific documents they were never trained on. Retrieval-
Augmented Generation solves this by combining two systems: a retriever and a
generator.

The retriever's job is to search a knowledge base — often a collection of
documents converted into vector embeddings — and find the passages most
relevant to a user's question. This is typically done using a vector database
such as FAISS, Pinecone, or Chroma, which stores high-dimensional numeric
representations of text and allows fast similarity search.

The generator is usually a large language model. Instead of answering purely
from its training data, the model is given the retrieved passages as context
and instructed to answer using that context. This grounds the model's output
in real, verifiable source material, significantly reducing hallucination
compared to an ungrounded chatbot.

A typical RAG pipeline has five stages: ingestion (loading and cleaning raw
documents), chunking (splitting long documents into smaller overlapping
pieces so retrieval is precise), embedding (converting each chunk into a
vector using an embedding model), indexing (storing those vectors for fast
search), and finally retrieval plus generation at query time.

Chunk size and overlap matter a lot in practice. Chunks that are too large
dilute relevance during retrieval; chunks that are too small lose context
that spans sentences. A common starting point is 500-1000 characters per
chunk with 10-20% overlap between consecutive chunks, tuned based on the
document type.

RAG is widely used in enterprise settings for building internal knowledge
assistants, customer support bots that answer from product documentation,
and legal or medical assistants that must cite their sources rather than
answer from memory alone.
