Vector Embeddings and Similarity Search

An embedding is a way of representing a piece of text — a word, sentence, or
paragraph — as a list of numbers (a vector) in a high-dimensional space. The
key property of a good embedding model is that pieces of text with similar
meaning end up close together in that space, even if they don't share the
same words. For example, "the stock market fell sharply" and "equities
suffered a steep decline" would produce vectors that are close together,
because an embedding model captures semantic meaning, not just keywords.

Sentence-transformer models such as all-MiniLM-L6-v2 are popular for this
task because they run locally on a CPU, are fast, and produce reasonably
strong embeddings for general-purpose text without requiring a paid API.
Larger models or API-based embeddings (such as OpenAI's text-embedding-3)
can produce higher-quality vectors at the cost of speed, price, and an
external network dependency.

Once text is converted into vectors, similarity search finds the vectors
closest to a query vector, usually using cosine similarity or Euclidean
distance. FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search) is an open-source library
that makes this search extremely fast, even across millions of vectors, by
using approximate nearest-neighbor algorithms instead of brute-force
comparison.

In a RAG system, at query time the user's question is embedded using the
same embedding model used for the documents, and the vector store returns
the top-k most similar document chunks. Choosing k involves a tradeoff:
too few chunks may miss the answer, too many chunks add noise and cost to
the generation step.
